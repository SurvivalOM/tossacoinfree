Is a coin flip really random?

A real, physical coin toss is famously close to fifty-fifty, though a well-known study suggests a coin very slightly favours the side that started facing up. A digital flip avoids that bias entirely: each result here is drawn independently, so past flips never influence the next one. Ten Heads in a row does not make Tails "due" — the odds reset to fifty-fifty on every toss. That is the gambler's fallacy, and this tool is immune to it.

A short history of the coin toss

People have let a spinning coin decide things for more than two thousand years. The Romans called it navia aut caput — "ship or head" — after the images stamped on their coins. Since then, coin tosses have chosen the kickoff in football matches, settled which brother flew first at Kitty Hawk, and even helped name at least one major city. The appeal never changes: when two choices feel evenly balanced, a fair coin turns an endless argument into an instant, impartial answer.
